Greece: Shadows of the Past
Photogrammetry - AliceVision Meshroom
Render Engine - Blender Cycles

Is it possible to escape the shadow of the past in Greece today?

The reality is that the Greek state has already erased certain aspects of its own past to create a homogenous and unifying memory that dominates public perception of Greece, both locally and abroad. To escape the undesirable shadows of its past, Greece has attempted to establish a national identity around a continuous history from Classical, Byzantine, to Modern Greece, selecting certain memories of the past while ignoring all others. Whether it is through the removal of non-Classical structures on the Athenian Acropolis or the forced renaming of towns from Slavic to Greek names, these attempts have largely demonstrated success in shaping perceptions by the average individual. This work walks through a photogrammetric reconstruction of the Ancient Athenian Agora and the adjacent Adrianou street, a site where historical and modern strata meet in tension. By shifting and dissolving these strata, the work visualizes the steps the modern Greek State has already taken to curate its historical narratives and exposes the voices and cultural vibrancies that will be overwritten by a continuation of such efforts.
Sanitization of Greek History
In its efforts to root its nation's foundations in the Classical period, the Greek state has metaphorically and physically destroyed aspects from its past to create a sanitized version that permeates popular culture both locally and internationally. The modern presentation of the Athenian Acropolis, for example, has stripped the site of any non-Classical or non-structural elements. Lacking the metal adornments, free-standing statues, polychomatic colors, and post-Classical buildings that should be visible, the current Acropolis is a barren, white-washed, curated image that has become the embodiment of Greece as a whole, one that attracts millions of tourists each year. The sanitization of the Acropolis is therefore a testament to the success Greece has had in escaping undesirable shadows of their past and constructing a national identity, at least in the eyes of international popular culture. As a result, however, these efforts often erase cultural vibrancies.

In recent years, there has been increased pushback against the destructive tendencies that arise from such historical sanitization. In 2019, the Greek courts blocked the demolition of two Art Deco era buildings on Dionysiou Areopagitou Street in Athens. These buildings were scheduled to be removed to clear a view from the New Acropolis Museum up to the Acropolis itself, a demolition that would have aligned with Greek's history of sanitization efforts. In other cases, such as the approved demolition of buildings on the corner of Makrygiannis 10 and Hatzichristou 21, these sanitizations are fulfilled.

Although the existence of these conflicts reveal internal conflict regarding this issue, they do not contradict the fact that Greece's sanitization efforts have proved successful when decisions are unanimous. New city plans have been designed with western grid systems with Neoclassical architecture that expunge any Ottoman influence. Similarly, efforts to reach a unified "Greek" identity have seen the forced renaming of rural villages from Slavic names to "Greek" names. Most impactful, perhaps, has been Konstantine Paparrigopoulos History of the Hellenic Nation from the Ancient Times Until the Modern, which traces an unbroken history from Classical, Byzantine, and Modern Greece. As a state-mandated text in public education, Paparrigopoulos' history has become a powerful unifying narrative, one that nevertheless largely ignores the role of Ottoman, Slavic, and other influences. These examples demonstrate that it is indeed possible to escape the shadow of the past in Greece when it is desired.

If decisions were unanimous and such sanitization efforts continue, what will happen to the excavataions at the Ancient Athenian Agora? Presently, excavations have slowed as the archaeolical site now extends below the modern street of Adrianou into the neighborhood of Monastiraki neighborhood. This work visualizes what possibilities might arise should the opinion of the Greek courts shift, demolishing the modern building facades in an explosion of points that reveal the potential archaeological remains that lie below.

Documentation | Process
281 images of the Ancient Athenian Agora were processed with photogrammetric software AliceVision Meshroom to produce a 3D mesh reconstruction. Unfortunately, the quality of the 3D mesh had to be decreased to improve computation time. As a result, the 3D mesh was then modified manually in Blender3D to replace missing pieces and to imagine how the Agora might extend below the modern street.
The 3D mesh reconstruction was then processed with CloudCompare to produce a uniform-density pount cloud.
The resulting pount cloud was then animated and rendered in Blender3D.





Documentation | Photogrammetry
Computation Time - 19 hours

Meshroom ran on UChicago's Peanut Cluster using 281 photos taken from the Ancient Agora in Athens, Greece. Non-research jobs are capped at 4 hours on the Peanut Cluster, so the photo set had to be broken into two, one for the Ancient Agora and one for the modern street, Adrianou. This ensured that distinct tasks could be run in under 4 hours.

The photo set included many sub-optimal photos that included tourists who changed positions across different images. To improve output, the Meshroom --overrides parameter was used to set the parameters for minNumOfConsistentCams to 2 and minNumOfConsistentCamsWithLowSimilarity to 3, under DepthMapFilter_1. Additionally, to fit the job within the time constraints of the Peanut Cluster, parameter describerPreset was set to "low" under FeatureExtraction_1. This reduced accuracy but improved computation time.

Trial runs using Google Colab were unsuccessful due limitations on GPU usages for free version users that disconnected the runtime when the CAPTCHA's failed.
Documentation | Animation
Render Time - 50 hours (~2:30 per frame for 1200 frames)

Video fly-through animated and rendered using Blender's Cycles. The point cloud was imported using Point Cloud Visualizer add-on with Blender3D Version 2.83.0 (up-to-date versions were incompatible).
